Sparks Fly at GiGse When Caesars Takes Aim at PokerStars

Posted on April 29, 2013 by Diamond Flush

Thousands of speakers, delegates and news media personnel descended on San Francisco last week for the Global iGaming Summit and Expo. An unexpected lively panel on day one kept the entire contingent buzzing all week.

The panel discussion was set to discuss licensing suitability, including the legal and commercial rationale of excluding off- shore operators. The panel included John Pappas, executive director of the Poker Players Alliance, Jan Jones-Blackhurst, senior Vice President of Communications and Government Relations of Caesars Entertainment and Michael Rumbolz, Management Committee of Seminole Hard Rock International.

It was Jan Jones that came out swinging.  Moderator Chris Krafcik, Research Director of Gambling Compliance, had barely introduced the topic when Jones began on her mission to make clear her position that any company operating in the United States market after UIGEA was passed in 2006 should not be welcome in a regulated market now.   She claimed that many people were wrong about UIGEA in thinking that it referred to just the monetary side of transactions, that instead, UIGEA made all operations of online gaming in the U.S. illegal.

Pappas came back explaining that UIGEA should not be construed to be the determining factor for who is licensed or not, that the regulatory agencies are capable of investigating and deciding who is suitable for licensing.    

She pointed out that while Nevada’s state legislature had seen fit to include a “bad actors clause” in their passage of an online gaming law, that she was disappointed that New Jersey did not have such language in their bill that passed in February. It wasn’t long before she actually named PokerStars as the object of her venom. PokerStars is currently waiting for license approval from the Casino Control Commission in New Jersey to purchase the Atlantic Club Casino which has been struggling to stay afloat.

Pappas came back explaining that UIGEA should not be construed to be the determining factor for who is licensed or not, that the regulatory agencies are capable of investigating and deciding who is suitable for licensing. He pointed to surveys that the P.P.A. had conducted of players as to whether they believe that PokerStars would be a welcome site on which to play if licensed in the U.S., the results of which were that an overwhelming majority would like to see a regulated PokerStars online site on which to play in the US. Pappas further made clear that the PPA supported PokerStars application for licensing, but certainly not to the exclusion of others, as Jones was suggesting.

Mike Rumbolz made clear that although he had no horse in the race in New Jersey, that he was a believer that the regulatory agencies in the jurisdictions involved could adequately investigate and determine who was suitable.

Jones nodded in agreement, but then further supported that the Nevada legislature had taken away that determination away from the Nevada Gaming Commission by virtue of the “bad actors” clause, which would prohibit any sites that operated in the U.S. after December 2006 from receiving a license for at least 5 years. She stated that companies other than PokerStars, such as Party Poker, 888, bwin and others, had seen fit to leave the US market post UIGEA (2006) rather than jeopardize their licenses. She also claimed that regulators have been “comfortable dealing with some U.S. operators for years” as if they should automatically be licensed without question. She continuously made statements about the integrity of these sites, and how that should be the determining factor as to licensing.

Not to be deterred, Pappas quoted to Jones from a white paper   issued by the American Gaming Association, that said explicitly that the AGA  believes that UIGEA “does not define illegal online gambling”. The AGA filed a petition in New Jersey to request standing to be heard in opposition of PokerStars application for licensing for a brick and mortar casino. Caesars, a longstanding member of AGA, is believed in the community to be one of the forces behind the petition. Although Pappas made the point of the AGA’s position on UIGEA several times, Jones never responded.

Facts be Damned?

Contrary to Jones assertions that, via UIGEA,  Congress said “the internet is illegal in the U.S.” , the UIGEA never made any such determinations. In fact, the  first section in the statute reads:

“Rule of Construction: No provision of this subchapter shall be construed as altering, limiting or extending any Federal or State law or Tribal-State compact prohibiting, permitting or regulating gambling within the United States”.

UIGEA is an enforcement act, relying on underlying predicate offenses, such as IGBA, Wire Fraud, Bank Fraud, etc.

As previously reported at Diamond Flush Poker, several of the companies that Jones aligns herself with, including those she mentioned when speaking about the integrity of companies that left the US market post 2006, could potentially also be considered “bad actors” if the post 2006 date did not exist, as it does in Nevada.  For example, Party Poker, to avoid criminal prosecution, agreed to forfeit $ 105 million to the US government in 2009 for its prior “bad acts” and admitted to being guilty of committing felonies by violating the the Illegal Gambling Business Act, Wire Fraud and Bank Fraud.   The CEO of Caesars Interactive, was the CEO of Party at the time that UIGEA was passed.  That same Caesars individual was also previously the CEO of Firepay, a company who processed payments for sportsbetting and casino games, which have never been legal in the US, either before or after UIGEA. Additionally, another individual that worked at Firepay at the time in question, now heads up Optimal Payments, who also made an agreement  with the US government in 2009, that besides forfeiting more than $ 19 million, included an admission to violations of IGBA and the Wire Act. Optimal is also the owner and operator of Neteller, whose co- founders were arrested and pled guilty to conspiracy to conduct illegal internet gambling transactions. Caesars Interactive recently formed a partnership with Optimal in anticipation of their future online gaming endeavor.

This author was the only person in the room to attempt a question to Jones during the Q&A period.  The question was supposed to be why Jones believed that the 2006 date was such an important one to determine licensing suitability, when companies and individuals with which she had aligned herself with, had pled guilty to certain felonies, including IGBA, wire fraud, bank fraud prior to 2006, while PokerStars had not had to admit to any wrongdoing at all as part of their settlement with the US DOJ just this past year.

First Jones interrupted the question to say that UIGEA was clearly the “flashpoint” to determine the illegality of all things internet gaming, and then said she didn’t understand the balance of my question at all.  Upon my clarification to her that UIGEA is an enforcement act and that IGBA and other laws were the underlying felonies that those she felt were suitable had pled guilty to, she admitted she did not know what the Illegal Gambling Business Act was, nor had she any idea what I was talking about as to those laws or any correlation to any companies or individuals other than PokerStars. She then alleged that I was trying to confuse her with some semantics game.

The session was called to a close and no further answer from Jones was forthcoming.

“The lady doth protest too much, methinks”. 1

If Jones believes, as she said,  that regulatory agencies should be the one making decisions as to who is suitable for licensing in their jurisdictions, why would it be necessary to applaud legislators, who are not the investigatory bodies, for taking that decision out of the hands of those regulators?

Why should 2006 be some magic date to determine who is guilty of violating certain laws, when the very companies she espouses should be licensed, admitted to committing illegal acts prior to that date?

If Caesars and other companies will offer internet gaming to the public when it becomes possible in New Jersey, Nevada and elsewhere, why fear the competition of other online gaming companies? The players will decide in the end where they will play.

After hearing from Jones, if there was any doubt as to whether Caesars backed the AGA action in New Jersey to stop PokerStars from being licensed, there no longer should be. New Jersey took no measure to include a bad actors clause in its online gaming bill because the Department of Gaming Enforcement and the Casino Control Commission would conduct the appropriate reviews of all current and future licensees. Hopefully this will happen with even criteria for all.
