SDNY, Campos and Elie: Final motions in limine filed, Past Acts and PartyGaming evidence in question

Posted on March 19, 2012 by Diamond Flush

With the start of the criminal trial just a few weeks away, the government and the defendants, Chad Elie and John Campos have filed their final pre-trial motions in limine. These motions are required to allow the court to decide which evidence will be permitted to be presented in front of the jury.

The government seeks permission to offer evidence at trial regarding Elie’s background in internet marketing before becoming involved with processing poker transactions,  Elie’s alleged theft of $ 4 million from PokerStars, Campos’ solicitation of a $10 million investment in Sunfirst Bank, Campos’ receipt of a $20,000 bonus for processing third party transactions at the bank, and Campos’ receipt of consulting fees from the bank as a member of it’s board of directors.  The government also asks the court to stop the defense from presenting evidence or arguments regarding the game of poker, or discussing any motives of the prosecution.

The defense motion asks the court to preclude the government from offering  any evidence related to Elie’s alleged theft of $ 4 million and also to bar the government from offering any evidence related to PartyGaming’s withdrawal from the U.S. market following the passage of UIGEA.           Continue reading →

Elie, Campos, and SDNY Issue Responses to Judge or Jury Motions

Posted on March 4, 2012 by Diamond Flush

The question whether the jury can hear evidence to decide if internet poker is covered by UIGEA and IGBA, and whether defendants Chad Elie and John Campos can argue a “good faith” or “advice of counsel” defense, is now in the hands of Judge Lewis Kaplan, who is presiding over the Black Friday criminal trial in New York.  At a January pre-trial conference, both the government and defense suggested that these issues would need to be resolved by the court prior to jury selection.  A ruling by the court in either direction would affect opening statements, the possible testimony of expert witnesses, jury instructions and the tone of the trial in general.  The defense and the government submitted their motions in limine  to the court last week, and their responses are now on the record as well.

Government Response

The government maintains it’s stance that the level of skill in online poker is irrelevant from an evidentiary standpoint. While prosecutors apparently agree with the defense motion that each offense element is a matter for the jury as the triers of fact, they contend it is a jump for the defense to then say that IGBA and UIGEA contain offense elements which depend on whether skill or luck “predominates” in poker, since the government contends that underlying state gambling law clearly does apply to poker.       Continue reading →

Elie, Campos: Jury Should Decide if Poker is Unlawful Gambling; SDNY: It’s the Court’s Job as a Matter of Law

Posted on February 27, 2012 by Diamond Flush

Chad Elie and John Campos have filed a “motion in limine” requesting permission to present evidence to the jury “concerning the characteristics of online peer-to-peer poker and defendants’ good faith and knowledge”. The government has filed their motion in limine supporting their belief that allowing such evidence would be counterproductive because the law is already clear that online poker constitutes illicit gambling, and that the charges do not rely upon a showing of willfullness on the part of the defendants.

The Defense Position

With the criminal trial fast approaching, attorneys for Elie and Campos have filed a motion with the court asking that the jury be permitted to hear evidence showing that peer-to-peer online poker would not fall under the parameters of the Unlawful Internet Gambling Enforcement Act (UIGEA) or the Illegal Gambling Business Act (IGBA).

The defense wishes to present such evidence to directly challenge the government’s claims that online poker meets the test for 1). “unlawful Internet gambling” and a “game subject to chance” under UIGEA, and  2). the  definition of “gambling” as prohibited by IGBA. The defense contends that the government must prove to a jury that online poker is “unlawful Internet gambling” because it is a “game subject to chance.” The government also has the burden to prove that “gambling”, as specifically described in the statute, includes online poker. In addition, Elie and Campos also requested to present evidence about “their knowledge and good faith beliefs” as to whether online poker met those tests, and its lawfulness.         Continue reading →

Campos and Elie submit briefs re: Wire Act opinion

Posted on February 7, 2012 by Diamond Flush

Background

On December 23, 2011 the Department of Justice Office of Legal Counsel (“OLC”), made public their opinion that the Wire Act  applies only to interstate transmissions of wire communications of bets or wagers on any “sporting event or contest”.1 Such an opinion is a contradiction to the long-held interpretation by the USAO that the parameters of the Wire Act are more far-reaching, and in fact, they have been used for decades in prosecutions of gambling activities.

On January 26, the judge in the Black Friday criminal case ordered that the parties submit additional briefs to address any implications that the recent DOJ legal opinion regarding the Wire Act’s parameters might have in the case.
