Baltimore Feds Indict Online Gambling Magnate Calvin Ayre, 3 others

Posted on February 28, 2012 by Diamond Flush

One day after the seizure of the Bodog.com domain by the U.S. Department of Homeland Security was leaked to the public, the Maryland United States Attorneys Office followed up with a press release announcing the  unsealing of an indictment against Bodog Entertainment Group, it’s founder, Calvin Ayre, and three other fellow Canadians and co-conspirators.

Maryland U.S. Attorney, Rod Rosenstein, made the public announcement this morning, joined by agents from I.C.E. and the I.R.S. “Sports betting is illegal in Maryland, and federal law prohibits bookmakers from flouting that law simply because they are located outside the country”, said Rosenstein.        

William Winter, Special Agent in Charge of ICE HSI in Baltimore. added “today’s indictment of Bodog Entertainment Group S.A. and its founder and operators sends a strong deterrent message to those that facilitate illegal online sports betting operations and commit crimes against our nation’s financial system.The proceeds from illegal Internet gambling are sometimes used to fuel organized crime and support criminal activity. ICE HSI, together with our law enforcement partners, will disrupt and dismantle organizations that commit these crimes, regardless of their location, whether here in the United States or abroad”.  I.R.S Special Agent Eric Hylton said “Laundering money from illegal activity such as illegal internet gambling is a crime. Regardless of how the money changes hands – via cash, check, wire transfers or credit cards – and regardless of where the money is stored – in a United States financial institution or an offshore bank – we will trace the funds. IRS Criminal Investigation will vigorously investigate and recommend prosecution against the owners and operators of these illegal enterprises to the fullest extent possible.”

The indictment charges Bodog Entertainment Group S.A., Calvin Ayre, 50, James Phillip, age 58, David Ferguson, and Derrick Maloney with violations of the Illegal Gambling Business Act (IGBA), and conspiracy to commit money laundering, 18 U.S.C. 1956.

The indictment alleges that Ayre and his colleagues moved at least $ 100 million in gambling winnings via checks and bank wires through payment processors, namely JBL Services and Zip Payments, Inc.,  to customers in the U.S., including Maryland.

The indictment also points to an unnamed media reseller, who is alleged to have created and executed an advertising campaign designed to “increase the participation of gamblers in the United States on Bodog.com”. This media reseller then billed Bodog Entertainment Group. S.A., d.b.a. Bodog.com, for this service, and in return received wire transfers from foreign banks, into U.S. banks totaling more than $ 42 million between 2005-2008.

Although the Bodog.com domain has not been active for customers of Bodog related licensees for some time, visitors now see the all too familiar seizure announcement including the logo of the U.S. Department of Justice and a Homeland Security Investigations shield, with the following announcement:

This domain name has been seized by the U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement-Homeland Security Investigations, Office of the Special Agent in Charge, Baltimore, Md, in accordance with a warrant obtained with the assistance of the U.S. Attorney’s Office for the District of Maryland, and issued pursuant to 18 U.S.C. 981 and 1955 (d) by the U.S. District Court for the District of Maryland.

It is unlawful to conduct an illegal gambling business in violation of 18 U.S.C. 1955 and any property used in violation of that section is subject to seizure and forfeiture pursuant to 18 U.S.C. 1955(d).

Ayre claims to have retired from Bodog to concentrate on his charitable foundation, although he still holds an interest in the brand licensing. The brand is now said to be under the control of Morris Mohawk Gaming Group in Canada.  The U.S. operations, since December 2011, operate under the name Bovada.

In addition to requesting forfeiture, jointly and severally, for any proceeds from the violations, the defendants each face a maximum of 5 years in prison on the IGBA charge and up to 20 years for the money laundering conspiracy. Bodog.com further faces a maximum fine of $500,000 on each of the two counts.
