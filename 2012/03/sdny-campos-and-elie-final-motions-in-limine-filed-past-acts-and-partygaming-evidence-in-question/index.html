SDNY, Campos and Elie: Final motions in limine filed, Past Acts and PartyGaming evidence in question

Posted on March 19, 2012 by Diamond Flush

With the start of the criminal trial just a few weeks away, the government and the defendants, Chad Elie and John Campos have filed their final pre-trial motions in limine. These motions are required to allow the court to decide which evidence will be permitted to be presented in front of the jury.

The government seeks permission to offer evidence at trial regarding Elie’s background in internet marketing before becoming involved with processing poker transactions,  Elie’s alleged theft of $ 4 million from PokerStars, Campos’ solicitation of a $10 million investment in Sunfirst Bank, Campos’ receipt of a $20,000 bonus for processing third party transactions at the bank, and Campos’ receipt of consulting fees from the bank as a member of it’s board of directors.  The government also asks the court to stop the defense from presenting evidence or arguments regarding the game of poker, or discussing any motives of the prosecution.

The defense motion asks the court to preclude the government from offering  any evidence related to Elie’s alleged theft of $ 4 million and also to bar the government from offering any evidence related to PartyGaming’s withdrawal from the U.S. market following the passage of UIGEA.          

Government Motion

The government asks permission to present evidence of Elie’s background in internet marketing, including his connection to prior payment processing for various internet shopping clubs and payday loan companies, while engaging in “dubious and misleading practices” marketing products to consumers that involved recurring billing, through which Elie reaped significant financial rewards.  The motion contends that it was in this prior position that Elie worked with Curtis Pope 1 connecting the payday loan company run by Pope to Elie’s membership shopping clubs. During that relationship, Pope introduced Elie to Daniel Tzvetkoff 2 the owner of Intabill, who had been processing poker transactions for Full Tilt Poker and PokerStars, presenting Elie as someone who had connections to numerous banks and someone who may be able to help disguise Intabill’s poker transactions with the banks. According to the motion, even after Elie began working with the poker companies, he continued to “engage in misleading marketing activities”. It was at this time that Elie partnered with Jeremy Johnson, 3 and eventually Elie and Johnson shared in the payment processing for internet marketing through SunFirst.

Elie and Johnson opened an account through Intuit to process poker transactions, allegedly telling Intuit and National Bank of California that the account would be used for payday loan transactions. After millions of dollars had been processed for Intabill through this account, the bank and Intuit put a stop to the processing feeling they were misled as to the nature of the transactions.  The motion contends that $ 4 million in the account was due to Intabill (which Intabill owed to PokerStars) but that Elie, having signatory rights in the account, transferred that $ 4 million, in three transactions, to an account he alone controlled. Even after being threatened by a mafia associate to return the money, Elie did not.  Intabill, assigned the debt to PokerStars, and reportedly Isai Scheinberg, head of PokerStars, reached out personally to Elie to repay the money. After Elie still resisted, PokerStars, via a proxy, sued Elie for the return of the money. Eventually a settlement was reached with Elie returning some of the money and then introducing PokerStars to SunFirst Bank.

The government also asks the court to allow evidence that Campos personally recommended that SunFirst Bank accept poker processing transactions in return for a $ 10 million capital investment by Campos’ friend Jeremy Johnson and Johnson’s partner, Chad Elie, who co-owned Elite Debit. Campo’s allegedly worked out a way to structure the infusion in such a way that the value of the investment would not require regulatory approval, i.e. greater than a 10% interest in the bank. In spite of alleged warnings to SunFirst from the Federal Trade Commission about the risks of third party processing, Campos continued to support the processing for Elite Debit including for poker transactions. Campos also allegedly solicited additional investment from Johnson for the bank and asked for and accepted a $20,000. bonus payment from Johnson for “Check and Credit Card Processing Consulting”. Campos reportedly kept $ 4500 of the money and distributed the balance to bank employees who were involved in the poker processing.

The motion goes on to point to additional funds that they claim Campos acquired through SunFirst, not only for his own personal gain, but to aid in keeping SunFirst afloat. SunFirst was eventually seized by regulators in November 2011 for being under-capitalized.

The government points to numerous decisions that could allow such background evidence of a charged offense to be admissible. Notably Federal Rule of Evidence 404 would prohibit evidence of a person’s character or character traits, but Rule 404(b) allows the following exception, on which the government relies:

(2) Permitted Uses; Notice in a Criminal Case. This evidence may be admissible for another purpose, such as proving motive, opportunity, intent, preparation, plan, knowledge, identity, absence of mistake, or lack of accident.

The government also asks the court to preclude the defense from offering any improper evidence or argument regarding the game of poker, or the motives of the prosecution. The government concedes that while poker is a popular game, and likely played by many members of the jury pool, the defense should not be be permitted to introduce evidence relating to the popularity of the game, the differences between poker and other forms of gambling, and the “unfairness” of restricting internet poker when other forms of gambling are allowed in other contexts or the fact that bills have been introduced to regulate and legalize internet poker. The government asserts that such arguments are improper as the only issue for the jury should be whether a defendant, engaged in the conduct that they are charged with, ie operating, aiding and abetting the operation of the poker companies, conspiring to commit money laundering or committing bank and wire fraud, and their own intent and knowledge to do so. Any other argument about poker would be irrelevant and prejudicial, and would, instead, ask jurors to determine whether internet poker should be illegal in the first place, sending the message that the jury should find for acquittal if they believe it should not be illegal.

In addition the government asks that the government’s motives for conducting the investigation and prosecution not be an issue for the jury. Such argument, says the motion, is not relevant to the guilt or innocence of a defendant. The government points to, among other cases, U.S. v Stewart, 4where the court rejected defense efforts to present arguments that could allow the jury to consider the motives of the government in deciding to investigate and prosecute Stewart. The court also found that eliciting evidence from the witnesses against Ms. Stewart regarding the government’s eagerness for their testimony, does nothing but raise the question of prosecutorial bias, which is not an issue for the jury. The popularity of poker, nor it’s hierarchical status above other forms of gambling are not relevant as to the question of whether the defendants violated the law, says the motion.

The same should apply to whether or not there is proposed legislation to legalize internet poker. Although the legislature may be considering new laws, this is not relevant as to whether the defendants broke existing laws and could entice the jury to acquit based on the fact that internet poker may become legal soon anyway. Therefore, such argument or evidence should be disallowed.

Defense Motion

The defense motion in limine concentrates on two points that the government may try to raise. The first is the $ 4 million that Elie allegedly stole from PokerStars via Intabill.  While Elie concedes that he was introduced to Intabill via Curtis Pope, and he did work with Intabill as the manager of their payday loan company, Hugo Services LLC, and he and Intabill both used the same company and a number of it’s affiliates to process transactions through Viable Marketing, he did not work with Intabill to process fraudulent transactions for the poker companies. The relationship that Elie had with Intabill and Pope and others that soured, involved the processing of transactions for payday loans and other businesses that had nothing to do with poker.

Regardless of the government’s zealous recounting of the dispute of the $ 4 million, the motion says, “the point remains that it is not relevant to any of the crimes charged in this case”. Furthermore, Elie’s alleged theft of the money cannot be admissible as an act in furtherance of the charged conspiracies, since even if they were related, such an act would not further the conspiracy but instead, would undermine it. If Elie had stolen money from Intabill after, as the government contends, he had been assisting Intabill in processing poker transactions, it would be impossible to make the leap that such an action would be designed to promote the business of processing for those same poker companies.

Even if the alleged theft of the $4 million had some probative value, under Rule 403, its minimal relevance would be vastly outweighed by the risk of unfair prejudice to Elie. Such evidence being introduced at trial would also force a “mini-trial” regarding the dispute between Elie, Pope, Intabill and Impact and the merits of his claim to the money in order to prove to the jury that he is not a thief. Rule 403 also permits the court to exclude relevant evidence that could confuse or mislead the jury or waste time. The waste of time relates to the fact that Elie would be forced to mount a substantial defense to refute the accusation that he “stole” funds from the bank account. Such a defense would require additional witnesses, as well as providing the jury a full picture of the complex financial relationships he had with each of the partners and affiliates involved. Not only would this mini-trial prolong the proceedings, it clearly would distract the jury from the real elements of the case.

Finally, the defense asks the court to preclude the government from making any reference or introduce any evidence relating to PartyGaming. Contrary to the government’s contention that with Party pulling out of the US market following the enactment of UIGEA , other sites “rushed to fill the void” by any means available, such an argument is irrelevant to any issues in this case. Furthermore, by allowing any reference to Party leaving the market, the jury is invited to assume that any company not doing the same were violating the law. The unfair prejudice in such a risk substantially outweighs any possible probative value by it’s offering.

UIGEA was enacted in 2006 and PartyGaming pulled out of the US market and stopped accepting bets from U.S. customers thereafter. In 2009, as part of a settlement agreement with the government, Party admitted using deceptive means to process transactions including using payment processors that misrepresented the coding of credit card transactions and an intermediary that issued checks under the name of a marketing company to pay U.S. customers. The defense claims to have searched through more than one million documents in the government’s production and found none to suggest that Elie was aware of any of these facts at the time he allegedly engaged in the conduct at issue. There are also no allegations that Elie or any of his alleged co-conspirators were involved with Party in any way. Finally Party leaving the US market is not at all probative of the UIGEA’s existence, nor the bank fraud or money laundering conspiracies charged. Therefore, Party’s closing of it’s U.S. operations have no bearing on any fact or consequence of this case and should be excluded under Rule 402. Even if Party’s decision to leave the market had some plausible relevance, the evidence should also be excluded under Rule 403 given the risk that the jury may use it for improper purposes.

The trial is scheduled to begin in New York on April 9, 2012.
