Kentucky Judge Issues Forfeiture Order for 132 Domain Names

Posted on March 19, 2012 by Diamond Flush

Three and one half years after the Commonwealth of Kentucky began proceedings against  141 Domain Defendants in rem, Judge Thomas Wingate has  issued an Order of Forfeiture, seizing 132 1  domain names of internet gaming sites that he judged to be in violation of illegal gambling activity pursuant to Kentucky state statutes.

On March 8, 2012, Wingate ordered and adjudged:      

  1. The Court finds as fact that no party made any attempt to prove to the court that they installed and implemented software or devices to geographically block users from inside the Commonwealth of Kentucky from their “illegal, unregulated internet gambling websites”.
  2. The Court previously heard evidence from the Commonwealth and determined that probable cause existed to justify the seizure of the domain defendants.
  3. Seizures of the property in rem constitutes notice to any persons that may lay claim to their interest.
  4. No party has appeared with standing to contest the forfeiture or submit evidence.
  5. The Court finds that no lawful owner or claimant of the domain defendants has been identified or is identifiable.
  6. The Court finds that evidence presented by the Commonwealth does establish by a preponderance of the evidence, “indeed by overwhelming evidence”, that the domain defendants are gambling devices and gambling records in violation of Kentucky statutes and therefore can be forfeited.
  7. While the Court considers amendments to the Kentucky statutes that define said “devices”, the court believes that those definitions are only a broad definition and determines that the intent can and does include a domain name.
  8. The Court re-adopts and incorporates its previous conclusions of law and holdings contained in it’s original order of seizure, September 2008 Exhibit B; it’s findings of fact and conclusions of law, September 2008 Exhibit C; and Opinion and Order, October 2008, Exhibit D.
  9. The Domain Defendants, Exhibit A, are hereby forfeited to the Commonwealth of Kentucky.
  10. Plaintiff shall serve to both VeriSign and each Domain registrar a copy of this order.
  11. VeriSign and each registrar shall immediately transfer ownership of each domain defendant to the Commonwealth of Kentucky. The domain names must be unlocked and unencumbered for Plantiff’s use, transfer or direction so that disposition by the Plaintiff may proceed in accordance with KRS 500.090.
  12. VeriSign and the registrars are ordered to direct each domain to the IP address or addresses as will be directed by the Plaintiff or its counsel.

Although the Franklin Circuit Court entered the above order on March 8, there has been a pending appeal already filed with the Kentucky Court of Appeals since November 2011, with appellate briefs due on April 24, 2012.

Background

In August 2008, the Commonwealth of Kentucky brought a civil “in rem” forfeiture action against 141 domain defendants to “put an end to alleged illegal internet gambling in violation of Kentucky state law”. The case was sealed.  In September 2008, the Commonwealth asked that the original complaint be stricken and refiled a new complaint, with an amendment. On that same date, the trial court heard evidence from the Commonwealth, including their description of hundreds of man-hours spent by a police officer and his team, randomly accessing various internet “gambling websites”, successfully placing bets or playing casino type games from within the Commonwealth. Evidence was also provided to show that other websites, (not on the list of 141 domain defendants) were able to successfully block and deny access to computers within Kentucky. The court  found probable cause that the 141 domain defendants were “gambling devices” used within Kentucky in violation of KRS 528 in connection with illegal gambling activity, and issued an Order for Seizure of the Domain Names.

Having found that no “lawful claimants” appeared to assert their interest in the domain defendants and to qualify for the return of their property, the Court scheduled a  forfeiture hearing, notifying each of the registrars of the domain defendants.

In time for that hearing, attorneys for 9 of the domain defendants came forward, namely: playersonly.com, pokerhost.com,sbglobal.com, sportsbook.com, sportsinteraction.com, mysportbook.com and linesmaker.com (named by the court as “Group of 7), also goldenpalace.com and goldencasino.com (called by the court the “Group of 2″). The Interactive Gaming Council (IGC) came forth to represent the rights of the internet gaming community and the Interactive Media Entertainment and Gaming Association (iMEGA) came forward to represent the rights of some of it’s members who are owners of some of the domain names. The Poker Players Alliance, (PPA) asked the court’s permission to file an Amicus brief, on behalf of it’s Kentucky membership of over 13,000, basis being that some of the domain defendants are hosting only poker games, which being a game of skill, should not be considered illegal under Kentucky gambling law. The Internet Commerce Association (ICA) also sought leave to present an Amicus brief, based on it’s members interest in internet governance and domain name administration. Finally, Network Internet Solutions(NIS), asked permission to take part on all aspects of trial and appeals, but without submitting to the jurisdiction of the court.

For purposes of clarity, all subsequent court documents referred to the above (Group of 7, Group of 2, IGC, iMEGA, PPA, ICA and NIS) as “Opposing Groups and Lawyers”.

At a September 26, 2008 hearing, The Commonwealth objected to the standing any of the “Opposing groups and lawyers” in light of the fact that none had filed on behalf of the specific identity of the actual owners of any domain defendants, and then further objected to any of their written motions to intervene, motions to dismiss, or motions to vacate the seizure orders. The Court agreed to allow all to be heard, but subject to future consideration of standing. The Court then further asked for both sides to submit briefs on:

    1.  Standing
    2.  Jurisdiction of the court
    3. Nature or status of the domain names as property
    4.  Nature of the domain names as a “gambling device” as defined in KRS 528.10 5
    5. Exclusion of poker as gambling as defined by KRD 528.10

These motions were filed and all parties were heard on October 7,2008. The Judge issued his Order and Opinion on October 16, 2008:

  1. Motion to dismiss: Denied
  2. Motion to intervene by IGC: Denied
  3. Re seizure order of September 18,2008: to allow each of the domain defendants 30 days to install and implement software that will block or deny access to any users or consumers within the Commonwealth of Kentucky, and prove that this software is operational. If such proof is satisfactory, the court agrees to relinquish jurisdiction over that domain name.
  4. Once jurisdiction has been relinquished over any of the domain defendants that have adhered to the above, the Commonwealth is directed to serve notice to the registrars of those defendants that the prior seizure order was rescinded.
  5. Final hearing date scheduled for November 17,2008.
  6. The September seizure order remains in effect as amended by the above.

IGC and iMEGA went to the Kentucky Court of Appeals seeking a writ of prohibition. In January 2009, The Appellate court overturned the trial court on the basis that domain names are not “gambling devices” and therefore not subject to seizure. The other issues before it, namely standing of the appellants, jurisdiction, intellectual property, game of skill questions for poker, were not ruled upon.

The Commonwealth then appealed to the Kentucky Supreme Court, who in March 2010, reversed the Appellate court decision but sent the case back to the trial level, asking that the question of associational standing be properly addressed. The court found that the associations must identify at least one individual owner of a domain that would be harmed, and that such owner is a member of the association.The Supreme court also said that if a party with sufficient legal standing were to come forth, the court would be inclined to rule on the merits.

In December 2010, the trial court issued an “Opinion and Order of Abeyance” finding that although iMEGA may have some associational standing in a narrow sense (having members that own the domain names), the court sees no proof that associational standing can extend to an in rem proceeding. The court orders briefs to support this and holds the forfeiture order in abeyance while doing so.

In the September 2011 order, in the trial court, Judge Wingate finds, that although IGC and iMEGA have both proven at least limited associational standing, that, among other reasons, the actual owners of the domains should personally lay claim and not hide behind the associations to “fight their fight” and use those associations as both their “sword and their shield”, and therefore denied any claims that either association has standing to intervene.

The IGC filed a Notice of Appeal in the Court of Appeals in October 2011 and its civil appeal prehearing statement in November 2011.  The next day, IGC also filed a motion to stay the trial court proceedings. Judge Wingate denied that motion. IGC immediately filed a motion to stay the trial court proceedings with the Court of Appeals, which was also denied, allowing the circuit court proceedings under Judge Wingate to proceed, which led to the March 2012 order.  Briefs are due to the Kentucky Court of Appeals April 24, 2012.

 

  1. While the original seizure order was for 141 domain names, this forfeiture order claims only 132 names. Missing are FullTiltPoker.com, PokerStars.com, AbsolutePoker.com and UltimateBet.com, which are currently parties in rem in the SDNY case, for which the Commonwealth of Kentucky has filed a claim; Doylesroom.com, Truepoker.com, Bookmaker.com which have already been forfeited via a Maryland DOJ action in 2011; bodoglife.com which is named in a current action also brought by Maryland DOJ; and Microgaming.com. ↩
