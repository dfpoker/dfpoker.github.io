Campos and SDNY, Is a Deal really a Deal?

Posted on March 28, 2012 by Diamond Flush

It’s been rumored since yesterday that John Campos, the Utah banker charged with six felonies in the Black Friday criminal case, came to an agreement with the USAO in the Southern District of New York, to enter a guilty plea to one single misdemeanor charge in settlement of the case against him.

Today, when the parties went before Judge Lewis Kaplan to present the plea, they got a surprise when Kaplan did not readily accept it.

According to an Associated Press report, Kaplan queried the government: “You’re basically walking away from the prosecution?” and then proceeded to remind all parties that he has the right to reject any plea brought before the court.     

Concerned about the extreme downgrade of the plea compared to the original charges, especially in light that the defendant had admitted to processing $ 200 million in poker transactions through SunFirst Bank, where he served on the Board of Directors and was part owner, Kaplan ordered the prosecution to submit their pleading in writing on what basis the court should accept a plea to a misdemeanor in this case.

In defense of their position, AUSA Andrew Goldstein advised the court that the potential maximum jail term for Campos with the misdemeanor plea, six months, is the about the  same as Campos would have faced with a single felony charge.

AUSA Arlo Devlin-Brown, the head prosecutor in the case, told the court that the plea agreed to with Campos also took into consideration the government’s concern that the jury may have a problem with a guilty vote if the case went to trial, because of the possibility they could hear the various legal opinions that may be ruled  admissible.  According to the motions in limine, which were sub judice at time of the plea, legal opinions from well respected attorneys could have influenced Campos’ (and Chad Elie’s) knowledge and good faith belief, aka mens rea, at the time, that the processing for peer-to-peer online poker was not illegal.

Finally, after spending a year planning for trial, Devlin-Brown stated that the case really concentrated on people that “duped” banks into accepting gambling proceeds by “hiding them behind sham companies” and that Campos only came in at the “tail end of the conspiracy” (2009).

A sentencing hearing is scheduled for June 27, and that is when Kaplan plans to give his decision on whether to accept the plea as presented today.
