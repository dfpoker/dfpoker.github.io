Ramifications of the Trial That Never Took Place

Posted on March 27, 2012 by Diamond Flush

Yesterday, Chad Elie entered a guilty plea to one charge of conspiracy to commit bank fraud settling the criminal case against him. He agreed to accept a sentence of 6-12 months in prison, and forfeit $ 500,000. If he were to be found guilty of all charges at trial, violations of UIGEA and IGBA, conspiracy to commit bank fraud, conspiracy to commit wire fraud, and money laundering conspiracy, potential maximum penalties exceeded 50 years and forfeitures probably in the tens of millions of dollars.

Today, John Campos, the Utah banker charged with agreeing to process payments for poker transactions and allegedly accepting investments from the processors into the struggling SunFirst bank where he was an officer, is rumored to have made an agreement to plead guilty to a single misdemeanor charge to settle his case.  Campos faced money laundering charges along with violations of the UIGEA and IGBA. If found guilty of all charges, he could have been looking at a maximum sentence of over 30 years.        

Elie, 32, an important payment processor for the major online poker sites,  and Campos, 57, pled not guilty to all charges in April 2011. For the last year, their impressive legal teams have gone full speed ahead, challenging all charges and asking the court to allow, among other things, evidence and testimony that poker is a game of skill rather than chance, that poker does not fit the definition of gambling, that their clients had legal opinions that led them to believe their actions were within the law, and that the payments handled by the defendants were not in connection with “unlawful internet gambling”.

Directly challenging the applicability of the charges for UIGEA and IGBA to poker and putting these questions, and others,  before the jury was being watched closely by the poker community. The decision to allow such evidence, has been sub judice, with the court’s decision expected any day. With both defendants having made plea arrangements prior to learning the court’s rulings, it’s no longer necessary for Judge Lewis Kaplan to put his decisions on the record.

John Pappas, Executive Director of the Poker Players Alliance (PPA), when asked about the ramifications of the Elie plea,  offered the following comment:

“The PPA has long expressed the position that the game of poker is  competition between players in which skill predominates and therefore a game which should be treated differently from the typical games of chance found in casinos or with the lotteries. The PPA has fought for this position in courts and legislatures around the country. And so the PPA, like the rest of the poker community, was observing the legal developments in Messrs. Elie and Campos  cases with great interest.  The arguments concerning the legality of poker that he and his lawyers advanced were exceptionally well crafted, very persuasive and surely deserving of a full hearing. With that in mind the PPA is disappointed that their recent guilty plea removes an opportunity for a Federal Court to rule on these important legal issues concerning poker.

But the PPA recognizes that Mr. Elie and Mr. Campos must do what they believe is best for themselves and for their families. The difference between the reported terms of his plea bargain and the potential sentence he could have received had he litigated and lost is one of undeniably vast proportion. Perhaps this is at least partly so because of the strength of the arguments they raised. Regardless, the decision to take the case to trial or take the plea bargain is uniquely theirs and the PPA respects this personal decision.”

Another attorney, active in the online gaming arena and familiar with the case, said yesterday following Elie’s plea:

“This may indefinitely postpone a decision on whether poker is a game of skill.  The court no longer needs to reach the issue.”

One poker player with a five figure balance pending on Full Tilt spoke on condition of anonymity saying: ” While I am happy that Campos and Elie both received sweetheart deals, I still feel gypped that the DOJ doesn’t have to answer for the chaos they started on Black Friday. They should have had to stand in that courtroom next month and cringe when the jury said “not guilty”. Now we are back where we started, waiting for Congress in Washington DC to get their act together and legalize a game between adults that should never have been targeted in the first place.”

Another player that expatriated from the U.S. to an undisclosed foreign location to continue playing post Black Friday said: “I was forced to leave the country to continue playing poker, which is my full time job, in order to feed my family. Obviously the DOJ in New York knew they had no chance of winning that case in front of a jury of 12 average American citizens. Playing poker is illegal? Seriously? I guess after “Fast and Furious” and “Solyndra“, they couldn’t take another chance of putting more egg on their face.”

Patrick Fleming, aka “Skallagrim”, an attorney in private practice and also a member of the PPA Board of Directors offered the following:

The defendants had very good lawyers, who did an excellent job of preparing their arguments in court. Some of the arguments had been developed over the course of the last few years, but these attorneys brought those arguments into actual practice and they do deserve great credit for getting them into play.

The ramifications of something not happening, means that these issues are still open and they could easily come around again. The issue of poker being a game of skill is still being litigated in other areas by the PPA…We still have a pending case in Virginia on that issue and we are still waiting for the Supreme Court in South Carolina to issue an opinion on that specific issue. This obviously would have been a key case, a major felony trial, in federal court, in the Southern District of New York. It really would have been a pretty definitive opportunity to litigate these issues. But it won’t be the last opportunity, not unless somehow, someday,our politicians  get their act together and pass some new legislation. Until they do, all of these issues still exist and they won’t be fought in April, but they will be fought sometime, until the law changes.

Five men indicted on Black Friday, including the heads of PokerStars, Full Tilt Poker and Absolute Poker, are still fugitives and face many of the same charges that might have been addressed at this trial.
